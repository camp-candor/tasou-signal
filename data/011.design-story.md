### **The First Five Seconds (Above the Fold)**

The page doesn't load; it resolves. It fades in from black not as a website, but as an object viewed under duress. There is no clean background, only the high-resolution texture of foxed, yellow-brown paper, haunted at the edges by the dark blooms of water stains. A low, unsteady flicker, as from a dying gaslamp, makes the shadows of the page seem to writhe. The soundscape is immediate and oppressive: the low, electric hum of cicadas in a sweltering, unseen night, punctuated by the faint, wet sound of something dripping.

At the top, etched in a typeface of bleeding, spidery ink, is the title: **"The Blackwood Parish Almanac."** Beneath it, a living date adds to the oppressive sense of place: *"The 217th Day since the River Beckoned."*

The navigation is not a bar, but a series of labels that look scorched into the vellum, like brands on old wood: **The Survey**, **The Testaments**, **The Census**, **Whispers & Warnings**. In the top-right corner, a small, wrought-iron icon of a keyhole invites me. It does not say "Sign In." It says, **"Inscribe Your Name."**

My cursor is not an arrow. It is a small, ghostly orb of light, a will-o'-the-wisp. The air feels thick, humid, and heavy with unspoken history.

### **The Urge to Scroll (Unveiling the World)**

My gaze is drawn to the centerpiece: what appears to be a developing daguerreotype of a vast, untamed swampland. This is **The Survey**. Its "fog of war" is not a digital overlay, but a milky, chemical void at the edges of the image, where the landscape has yet to resolve. The only clear point is a small huddle of salt-bleached clapboard buildings clinging to a rickety dock over murky water: the town of **Salvation**, its icon a lantern casting a weak, pulsing golden light.

As my will-o'-the-wisp cursor drifts over the undeveloped haze, the milky surface swirls, revealing maddening, half-formed glimpses of what might lie within—a skeletal cypress knee, the jagged roofline of a sunken house—before they vanish back into the chemical fog. This interaction is not a simple hover effect; it is a struggle against a devouring obscurity. It's an irresistible invitation to see what's hidden, to scroll down and bring more of this doomed world into focus.

Scrolling feels less like moving a webpage and more like turning the heavy, vellum page of a massive ledger. The page flows into the next section, **"The Testaments,"** a collection of journal entries. They are not uniform cards in a grid, but fragments of letters and diary pages that look as though they’ve been pasted, stitched, or even nailed into the main body of the Almanac.

### **The First Interaction (The Call to Adventure)**

My first true interaction is with the map. I click on a discovered landmark—a crumbling plantation manor marked by a dark ink stain, like a pool of dried blood. The entire daguerreotype ripples, and a sound like a wet, indrawn breath is heard. A tattered card, as from a forgotten tarot deck, flips over, displaying its name and a grim status: `Forsaken`. The link is not a button. It is a single, cryptic phrase: *[Bear Witness]*.

I move down to the Testaments. One is a diary page in elegant, looping cursive, the ink bleeding with humidity. Another is a damp, yellowed telegram slip. Instead of a "Read More" link, there is a physical object: a wax seal bearing a skull. As my cursor—my wisp—hovers over it, the seal glows with a faint, internal heat. I click. The seal doesn't vanish; it cracks and crumbles to dust, revealing the full, harrowing account in a modal that feels like unfolding a fragile, secret letter.

Further down, in **"The Census of Blackwood Parish,"** I find the roster. The character portraits are stark, charcoal sketches, posed like Victorian post-mortem photographs. Their eyes seem to follow my cursor. I hover over one. It doesn't show me a quote. Instead, a single, haunting audio clip plays: a man's last recorded words, a desperate gasp cut short. Their status is not `Active` or `Retired`. It is `Wandering`, `Sheltered`, or `Given to the Parish`. The interaction is a punch to the gut. This is not a game of heroes. This is a record of the lost.

### **Lingering Impressions**

Long after I leave, the feeling of the Almanac remains. It is the texture of the stained paper, the sound of the cicadas, the oppressive weight of a history soaked in sorrow. The lingering image is of the Census, that grim ledger of souls, and the final invitation that floats like a ghost over the faded paper at the bottom of the page. It is not a call to adventure, but a somber challenge aimed directly at me:

**"The Almanac has empty pages. The Census has names yet to be entered. Will you offer your testimony?"**

It’s a question that implies a cost. Clicking it fades the world to black before a stark, formal sign-in page appears, as if I am finally signing my name in the parish's cursed ledger. The impression is not one of fun, but of profound and compelling dread.

## Design Rationale

As the Senior UX Designer, my goal was to create a homepage that is not a brochure *for* the game, but an extension *of* the game. This design is a direct synthesis of the provided context, explorations, and inspirations.

**1. A Diegetic Interface Aligned with the "Living Chronicle" Concept:**
The core context (`009.home-page-context.md`) specifies a West Marches campaign where the Astro site serves as a "Living Chronicle." The "Blackwood Parish Almanac" concept, drawn from `008.site-design.md`, is the perfect execution of this. The interface is diegetic—it is an in-world artifact. Session logs aren't blog posts; they are "Testaments." The character roster is "The Census." This narrative framing makes the out-of-game administrative function of the site feel like an integral part of the in-world experience.

**2. "Lyrical Decay": Synthesizing Aesthetics for Maximum Immersion:**
The design's aesthetic is a direct implementation of the "Lyrical Decay" concept identified in the "AMERICAN SOUTHERN GOTHIC ON SCREEN" analysis (`010.design-inspirations.md`). It fuses the elegant (serif typography, ornate icons) with the corrupting (water stains, bleeding ink, flickering light). This creates the central tension of "Beauty vs. Horror" also noted in the inspiration file, making the experience alluringly beautiful and deeply unsettling at the same time. The flickering gaslamp and cicada soundscape are designed to evoke an "Oppressive & Sweltering" atmosphere.

**3. Onboarding Through "Show, Don't Tell":**
A new player learns everything they need to know about the game's tone and mechanics without reading a single feature list.
*   **The Survey Map:** Its interactive "chemical fog" immediately communicates that this is a game about exploration and uncovering a hidden, dangerous world.
*   **The Testaments:** The varied, player-written entries demonstrate that the world is persistent and community-driven.
*   **The Census:** The grim status of `Given to the Swamp` instantly communicates that this world has real, permanent consequences.

**4. The Uncanny Interaction Model:**
The design intentionally subverts standard web conventions to create a sense of the "Uncanny," a key theme from the American Gothic analysis (`010.design-inspirations.md`).
*   The cursor is a will-o'-the-wisp, making the user an active, otherworldly participant.
*   Links are not buttons but "smudged thumbprints" or "cracked wax seals," transforming a simple click into a tactile, narrative action.
*   The final Call-to-Action, *"Will you offer your testimony?"*, frames joining the game not as a purchase or sign-up, but as a solemn act of participation in a tragic history. This is a powerful filter; it repels players looking for a casual power fantasy and strongly attracts those who are drawn to the game's unique, somber premise. It finds the right audience by asking the right question.

The contrast is jarring and deeply uncanny—a moment of rational, digital intrusion into an ancient, occult space.

4.  **Interaction as Effort:** The act of scrying into the map—requiring a click-and-hold to fight back the murk—makes accessing information a deliberate, physical effort. The sudden appearance of the stark, clean, modern Clerk sign-in form after being immersed in the analog, mystical Orrery is designed to be jarring. This break in the aesthetic makes the act of signing up feel significant and transgressive, like introducing a piece of the modern world into an ancient, sealed system.

## Design Rationale

As the Senior UX Designer, this alternative concept, "The Cathedral of Whispers," intentionally pivots away from the personal, humid horror of the "Almanac" to explore the grand, solemn, and architectural aspects of the Gothic tradition.

1.  **A Shift in Metaphor: From Artifact to Architecture:** The core decision was to replace the "found object" with an "explorable space." This directly pulls from the **"Fractured Cathedral Architecture"** aesthetic in "The Haunted Castle" inspiration file (`010.design-inspirations.md`). This shift changes the player's emotional journey from one of dread and decay to one of awe and solemn purpose. It better serves the West Marches principle (`009.home-page-context.md`) of a grand, communal effort to map a vast world.

2.  **Grandeur and Obscurity:** The design is built on the tension between the sublime scale of the cathedral and the overwhelming darkness (informational obscurity). This is a direct translation of the core tenets from "The Gothic Novel" brief (`010.design-inspirations.md`): a "fallen world" defined by "Chiaroscuro & Obscurity." The user feels small but part of something immense, and their primary task is to bring light (information) to the darkness (the unknown).

3.  **Elevating the "Living Chronicle":** The West Marches campaign is a "Living Chronicle." By presenting session logs as **illuminated manuscripts** and player characters as entries in a **Martyrology** (an idea from the unused "Cathedral" concept in `008.site-design.md`), we elevate player actions from mere game reports to sacred history. This fosters a sense of legacy and importance that is highly motivating for the target player.

4.  **Interaction as Illumination:** The core interactive mechanics are designed around the theme of light versus darkness. The user's cursor becomes a tool to illuminate manuscripts or move a candle across a map. This is a direct, interactive translation of the inspiration from "The Haunted Castle" (`010.design-inspirations.md`), where **atmosphere is an active agent** and moonlight is a "theatrical searchlight." It makes the simple act of discovery feel powerful and deliberate.

5.  **A Different Call to Adventure:** The final CTA, **"Will you add your chapter to the Great Work?"**, reframes the act of joining. The "Almanac" asked for testimony, an act of survival. This version asks for contribution, an act of creation and legacy. This appeals to the player's desire to leave a permanent mark on a persistent world, a core motivator in the West Marches style. The entire funnel is designed to make the player feel like a scholar or an acolyte joining a holy order, not just a player signing up for a game.

Great, dark fissures run across it like canyons, filled with a slow-moving, self-illuminating, **fuchsia ooze**. This unnatural, vibrant liquid pulses with a soft, internal light, its color a violent and sickly contrast to the muted decay of the landscape.

My cursor is not an arrow or an orb. It is a sharp, metallic glint, like the tip of a surgical scalpel. In the center of the screen, the title **GLOPRACHET** congeals from the fuchsia ooze itself, the letters forming and dissolving with a wet, biological uncertainty. This is not a website; it is an autopsy.

### **The Urge to Scroll (Unveiling the World)**

As I scroll down, I am not navigating a page; I am panning down the continent of a dead body. The camera pulls back slightly, and the "alabaster landscape" reveals its true nature: I was on the colossal, fossilized shoulder of a titanic humanoid entity. The scroll is a descent.

The fissures become veins. A massive, curved structure I scroll past is a petrified rib, large enough to form an archway over a dark swamp. The Testaments, the session logs, are not cards or pages. They are memories trapped in the god's leaking ichor. As I scroll, my scalpel cursor passes over rivulets of the fuchsia ooze that snake down the body. When it does, a ghostly daguerreotype portrait—a player's face—surfaces within the translucent liquid, their features momentarily clear before distorting and sinking back into the depths. Next to the rivulet, a single, harrowing sentence from their log is tattooed onto the skin of the god, written in bleeding, spidery ink.

Further down, I reach a vast, pitted field of hardened fuchsia crystals. This is the Census. Each crystal holds a trapped, screaming face, a character portrait frozen in a moment of terror. Hovering my scalpel over a crystal causes it to resonate with the low hum, and the character's name and status appear etched on its surface. The status of those `Given to the Swamp` is clear: their crystals are cracked and dark, the face within almost entirely obscured by the corrupted fuchsia growth.

### **The First Interaction (The Call to Adventure)**

My morbid curiosity drives me. I scroll back up to a large, pooling wound near the god's collarbone, a place where the fuchsia ooze is deep and wide. This is The Survey. I click, and my scalpel pierces the surface of the ooze. The hum intensifies.

I drag the cursor. The ooze does not part. Instead, the liquid becomes a scrying pool. Beneath the surface, a topographic map of the swampland resolves, projected in lines of dark, venous light. The interaction is hypnotic and repulsive. I am literally using the god's blood to chart the land that consumed it. Lifting my click causes the map to dissolve back into a meaningless, pink-purple swirl.

But the true call to action is at the end of the scroll, at the bottom of the descent. I arrive at the god's chest cavity, where the ribs part to reveal a massive, geode-like heart of black crystal. In its center is a pulsating, liquid core of pure fuchsia ooze. Floating above it, a challenge materializes, the words shimmering like a heat haze:

**THE BLOOD REMEMBERS. THE CENSUS THIRSTS.**

The only interactive element is the pulsating core itself. Moving my scalpel over it causes the hum to rise in pitch, becoming an expectant, hungry whine. Clicking is not an act of signing up; it is an act of commingling, of offering a blood sample to this monstrous archive. The screen flashes a violent fuchsia before cutting to a stark, clinical, black-and-white Clerk sign-in page, which now feels like a medical consent form for a deeply unholy procedure.

### **Lingering Impressions**

The image that is seared into my mind is the violent, beautiful, and utterly wrong color of the fuchsia ooze, pulsing against the dead, monochrome landscape of the god's body. The sound is the deep, somatic hum of something immense and ancient. I am left with the feeling of having been a trespasser, a pathologist, and perhaps a parasite. The world of this game is not just haunted by its past; it is literally built upon its corpse.

## Design Rationale

As the Senior UX Designer, this "Gloprachet" concept is a deliberate push into Weird Fiction, using the core tenets of Southern Gothic as a foundation for something more alien and unsettling.

1.  **Grandeur through the Grotesque:** The primary source of **Grandeur** is the scale of the dead god. This directly adapts the **"Ruined Edifice"** metaphor from the inspiration files (`010.design-inspirations.md`), but replaces the familiar crumbling castle with a monumental, biological equivalent. This creates a sense of awe that is intrinsically tied to horror and decay, fulfilling the "ruined beauty" pillar of the Southern Gothic aesthetic.

2.  **Obscurity through a New Medium:** Information is deliberately obscured. By making the **fuchsia ooze** the medium for the map and the container for memories, the design forces a sense of struggle. This is a direct translation of the design challenge from "The Gothic Novel" brief (`010.design-inspirations.md`): **"Clarity vs. Obscurity."** The user is never given enough information to feel secure; they must actively scry and decipher it from an unreliable, alien substance.

3.  **Integrating the "Fuchsia Ooze" as the Uncanny:** The fuchsia ooze is the critical element that prevents the design from being a generic "dark fantasy" concept. It is the **"Intrusion of the Real"** from the American Gothic analysis (`010.design-inspirations.md`). In a world defined by the natural decay of mud, rust, and bone, this vibrant, living, unnatural substance is a profound violation. Its "Lyrical Decay" is not historical but biological and alien, injecting a unique and memorable brand of terror into the familiar Southern Gothic framework.

4.  **Deconstructing and Re-contextualizing Raw Elements:** I deliberately took raw aesthetic components from `008.site-design.md` and fused them with this new metaphor. The "bleeding, spidery ink" is no longer on a page but tattooed on dead skin. The "daguerreotype" portraits are not on cards but are ghostly apparitions in the ooze. This adheres to the constraint of using the raw materials in a completely novel combination.

5.  **A Visceral Call to Action:** The West Marches style (`009.home-page-context.md`) demands player investment. The final CTA—offering blood to the Census—reframes the act of creating a character. It is not a simple sign-up; it is a thematic, sacrificial act. This aligns with the "Temptation/Falling" theme from "The Gothic Novel" brief (`010.design-inspirations.md`), where seemingly beneficial actions can have hidden, dire consequences. It creates a powerful first choice for the player before they have even entered the game proper.

The new core metaphor is not an object you hold or a machine you operate. It is a broadcast you stumble upon, late at night, in the middle of nowhere. It is **The Midnight Gospel Radio of Lickskillet.**

### **The First Five Seconds (Above the Fold)**

The page opens to near-total darkness and a wall of sound: the thick, humid drone of a million cicadas at the height of summer, layered with the low, warm hum of a vacuum tube and the unmistakable crackle of AM radio static. It is an oppressive, sweltering soundscape.

Slowly, a single point of light resolves from the gloom. It is the dim, orange-red glow of a vacuum tube inside an ancient Bakelite radio. The radio itself is the entire screen. Its casing is cracked like dried Georgia red clay, and a peeling Salvation Army sticker is barely visible on the side. The title, **"LICK SKILLET,"** is not a digital font; it's part of the radio’s original, faded branding on the tuning dial.

My cursor is not an arrow or an orb. It is a flickering, unstable mote of static, a visual representation of electrical interference. The only movement is the slow, rhythmic pulse of the glowing tube, a mechanical heartbeat in the suffocating dark.

### **The Urge to Interact (Unveiling the World)**

There is no scroll bar. The interface *is* the radio. My interaction is purely tactile. Two large, chunky knobs and a row of rusted toggle switches are the only clickable elements.

The large knob on the left controls the **Tuning Dial**. A tarnished brass needle sits above a curved window displaying the frequency band. The stations are not marked with numbers but with cryptic, hand-scrawled labels: *The Parish Roll Call. Congregation Testimonies. Local Almanac. Whispers in the Static.*

The knob on the right controls the **Volume**. Turning it up doesn’t just increase the sound; it brings the cicada drone and the oppressive humidity to the forefront, making the experience more claustrophobic. Turning it down reveals fainter, more unsettling sounds buried in the static—a floorboard creaking, a distant dog barking once.

The toggle switches are for filtering the broadcast. One is labeled *Signal*, the other *Noise*.

### **The First Interaction (The Call to Adventure)**

My hand is drawn to the Tuning knob. I click and drag. The sound is a deep, satisfying *ka-thunk* as the dial turns, accompanied by a rising and falling screech of static. The brass needle scrapes across the dial window. As I turn, I hear fragments of broadcasts—a snippet of a forgotten bluegrass song, a fire-and-brimstone sermon—before they are consumed again by static.

I tune the needle to **"Congregation Testimonies."** The static lessens, and a voice fades in. It’s not a polished recording; it sounds like a message left on an old answering machine, the speaker’s voice trembling.
> *"…we followed the old levee road to the Blackwood property. Elara… she was taken by something in the sugarcane field. We left her locket on the mantelpiece as a marker… a marker for those who follow… fled at dusk as the cicadas began their… their chorus."*
The message ends with a click and the hum of an empty line. These are the Testaments—the session logs—reimagined as found audio.

Next, I tune to the **"Local Almanac."** There is no visual map. Instead, the same gravelly voice of the station’s host—a Preacher or a Chronicler—describes the world in a slow, hypnotic cadence, his voice layered over the sound of wind through pine trees.
> *"The Survey for this week shows a deep blight moving in from the eastern swamps. The waters by the old Blackwood Manor are running black again. Word is the Ferryman Company lost a soul out there on Day 198. Be advised… the kudzu has swallowed the path to the sunken chapel. Only the bell tower remains, like a single, crooked tooth."*
This is The Survey—an atlas for the mind, built on obscurity and oral tradition.

The call to action is not a button. It is a toggle switch, separate from the others, with a wrought-iron guard over it. Next to it, a hand-etched label on a brass plate reads: **GO ON-AIR.** Flipping this switch is the only way to participate, to add your own testimony to the broadcast.

### **Lingering Impressions**

The sound haunts me. The Preacher’s voice, the crackle of the static, the oppressive wall of cicadas. The homepage is an auditory experience first and a visual one second. The lingering image is the single, pulsing vacuum tube—a lone, warm heart in an overwhelming darkness. I leave with the uncanny feeling that I have discovered a secret frequency, a ghost broadcast from a town that time forgot, and the deep-seated dread that something in the static was listening back.

## Design Rationale

As the Senior UX Designer, this concept is engineered to be a radical departure, fully committing to the most extreme interpretations of the source material to create an unforgettable experience.

1.  **Auditory-First Grandeur:** The "Midnight Gospel Radio" reframes **Grandeur** not as visual scale, but as atmospheric and auditory immersion. The vastness of the Southern night, the overwhelming presence of nature (the cicadas), and the profound loneliness of a single broadcast create a different kind of sublime experience. This is a direct implementation of the **"Oppressive Hum"** and **"Sudden Silence"** metaphors from the Southern Gothic inspiration (`010.design-inspirations.md`), making the soundscape the primary vehicle for terror.

2.  **Radical Obscurity and the Uncanny:** This design elevates **Obscurity** to a core mechanic. By removing visual maps and presenting information as unreliable, audio-only fragments, we force the player into a state of deep uncertainty. This aligns perfectly with the West Marches principle that **Information is a Player-Managed Resource** (`009.home-page-context.md`); here, players must listen intently and share notes to build a coherent picture of the world. The radio itself is a manifestation of **"The Uncanny"** from the American Gothic brief (`010.design-inspirations.md`)—a familiar object behaving in deeply unsettling ways.

3.  **A New Synthesis of Southern Gothic:** This concept is hyper-focused on the rural, folk-horror aspect of the Southern Gothic, moving away from aristocratic decay and into what the "Lickskillet" title suggests: poverty, grit, and superstition.
    *   The design curates raw elements from `008.site-design.md`—the "cracked mud" texture, the "spidery ink" font (as handwriting), the "wrought iron" (for the switch guard), and the "Whispers & Warnings" concept (as a radio segment)—and re-contextualizes them onto the radio artifact.
    *   It draws heavily on the core tension of **"Clarity vs. Obscurity"** from Tracy's "The Gothic Novel" (`010.design-inspirations.md`), as the user is constantly fighting the static to find a clear signal.

4.  **Narrative-Driven Interaction Model:** The entire UX is built around the narrative of tuning a radio. "Navigation" becomes "tuning." "Session logs" become "testimonies." This reframes the user's actions as in-world behaviors, dramatically increasing immersion. The final CTA, "GO ON-AIR," is the most powerful possible invitation for a player in a Living Chronicle campaign—it offers them a chance to become part of the broadcast, to add their voice to the history of Lickskillet. This is a direct, thematic fulfillment of the project's core goal.
